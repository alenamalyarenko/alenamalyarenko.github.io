The multi-scale effects of the meltwater in the Ross Sea range from disrupting stratification at the ice-ocean interface to potentially inviting deep warm waters into the Ross Ice Shelf cavity, causing the melting to skyrocket. Here I will talk about different aspects of the meltwater in the Ross Sea.

The buoyant meltwater at the ice shelf-ocean boundary disrupts our assumptions of a well-mixed boundary layer under a solid lid. Our observations, obtained through a 200m borehole through the Ross Ice Shelf, showed that the commonly-used ice shelf melting parameterisations are not valid in summers in this location. Further analysis suggested that presently there can’t be a one-fits-all parameterisation for the ice shelf-ocean melt, and LES boundary layer modelling is required to fix this problem.

The Ross Sea produces the saltiest water around Antarctica, known as High Salinity Shelf Water. However, since the beginning of regular observations in the 1960s, HSSW has been freshening. While interannual variability can disrupt the trend for short periods, it is assumed that the continued melting of the West Antarctic ice shelves will continue to freshen HSSW. Why do we care? Modelling studies have shown that eroding the volume of HSSW will eventually remove the density barrier between the Ross Ice Shelf cavity and the warm, deep Circumpolar Deep Water surrounding the continent. Once given the opportunity, CDW will enter the cavity and increase the melting tenfold. I’ll try to convince you to stay optimistic, but I make no promises.
